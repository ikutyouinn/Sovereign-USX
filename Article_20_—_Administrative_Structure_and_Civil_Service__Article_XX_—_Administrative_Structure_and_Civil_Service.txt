Article XX â€” Administrative Structure and Civil Service

Charge

This Article establishes constitutional principles governing the structure, authority, and operation of administrative bodies and the civil service within the Republic. Administrative authority shall be executive in nature, limited to the faithful implementation of law, and subordinate to constitutional governance, democratic accountability, and the rights of the People. Nothing in this Article shall be interpreted to authorize administrative sovereignty, independent policymaking, or the exercise of power beyond that expressly granted by law and subject to transparency and judicial review.

Section 1. Nature of Administrative Authority

a. Executive and Subordinate Character
Administrative authority is executive in nature and exists solely to carry out and implement lawfully enacted decisions of the constitutional branches.
Administrative bodies possess no independent sovereignty, legitimacy, or authority apart from that which is expressly granted by law.

b. No Policymaking or Lawmaking Power
No administrative body, office, or official may create, amend, suspend, interpret, or expand law, policy, or rights beyond explicit statutory authorization.
Administrative interpretation shall be limited to faithful execution and shall not substitute internal judgment, theory, or preference for enacted law.

c. Instrumental Role
Administration is an instrument of governance, not a governing authority.
Administrative bodies shall execute decisions made through lawful constitutional processes and shall not claim authority derived from expertise, professionalism, efficiency, necessity, or institutional continuity.

d. Prohibition on Independent Doctrine
No administrative body may adopt internal doctrines, frameworks, professional standards, academic theories, or ideological constructs as binding authority absent explicit legislative enactment.
Internal guidance shall not possess the force of law.

e. Non-Expansion by Practice or Custom
Administrative authority shall not expand through custom, precedent, internal practice, emergency habit, or historical usage.
Powers not expressly granted are reserved and withheld.

f. Transparency and Accountability
All exercises of administrative authority are subject to transparency, recordkeeping, and public accountability consistent with Article XI.
Administrative convenience shall not justify opacity, discretion without review, or insulation from oversight.

g. Subordination to Constitutional Limits
Administrative authority is always subordinate to the Constitution, the laws enacted thereunder, and the rights of the People.
Any administrative act inconsistent with constitutional limitation is void and shall be so declared by the Constitutional Review Court upon petition by any affected person, the CJC, or the Legislature.

Section 2. Civil Service as Public Stewardship

a. Civil Service as Trust
Civil service is a public trust exercised on behalf of the People.
Civil servants hold authority only as stewards and custodians of lawful public functions, not as independent actors or policy authorities.

b. Duty of Loyalty and Neutrality
Civil servants owe a duty of loyalty to the Constitution and the law, not to any political party, faction, ideology, doctrine, academic school, or private interest.
The execution of public duties shall be neutral, impartial, and faithful to enacted law.

c. Duty of Candor
Civil servants have an affirmative duty of honesty and candor in all official actions, records, communications, and representations.
Knowingly misleading the public, elected officials, courts, or oversight bodies constitutes a breach of public trust.

d. Service, Not Governance
Civil service exists to administer and implement decisions lawfully made by constitutional authorities.
Civil servants shall not govern, direct public outcomes, or substitute administrative judgment for democratic or judicial decision-making.

e. No Independent Public Mandate
Civil servants possess no independent mandate from expertise, professionalism, credentialing, or institutional role.
Public legitimacy flows solely from the Constitution and laws enacted thereunder.

f. Fiduciary Responsibility
Civil servants shall exercise care, restraint, and diligence in the use of public authority, resources, and information.
Misuse of authority for personal, institutional, ideological, or concealed purposes constitutes a breach of fiduciary duty.

g. Accountability to the People
Civil servants are accountable to the People through lawful oversight, transparency, and review.
Claims of internal discretion, professional judgment, or administrative necessity shall not defeat accountability.

Section 3. Limits of Administrative Power

a. No Implied Authority
Administrative bodies possess only those powers expressly granted by the Constitution or by law.
No power shall be implied from necessity, efficiency, expertise, tradition, or institutional practice.

b. Prohibition on Independent Rulemaking
No administrative body may promulgate rules, standards, guidance, or directives having binding effect absent explicit legislative authorization.
Administrative instruments lacking such authorization shall have no force of law.

c. No Expansion by Interpretation
Statutory ambiguity shall not be construed to enlarge administrative authority.
Where doubt exists, interpretation shall favor restraint and deference to legislative intent.

d. Emergency Authority Strictly Limited
Claims of emergency or exigency do not create new administrative powers.
Any temporary authority exercised under emergency conditions shall be narrowly construed and shall expire automatically absent timely legislative ratification.

e. Internal Policies Not Law
Internal manuals, policies, professional standards, best practices, or compliance frameworks shall not create enforceable obligations for the public absent express legal enactment.
Violation of internal policy alone shall not constitute legal wrongdoing.

f. No Authority by Delegation Chain
Administrative authority shall not be enlarged through cascading delegation, subcontracting, or internal redistribution of responsibility.
Delegated authority shall be construed narrowly and may not exceed the scope of the original grant.

g. Reservation of Power
All powers not expressly conferred upon administrative bodies are reserved to the People and the constitutional branches.
Silence shall not be interpreted as consent.
h. Emergency Authority Sunset
Any administrative authority exercised on the basis of emergency, urgency, or exigency shall expire automatically within a fixed period unless expressly ratified by the Legislature.

Successive or rolling emergency claims for the same or substantially similar purpose are prohibited absent a new and distinct legislative grant.


Section 4. Personnel Offices and Employment Administration

a. Limited Administrative Function
Personnel offices exist solely to perform neutral administrative functions related to public employment.
Such functions are limited to hiring logistics, credential verification, payroll, benefits administration, leave management, and maintenance of employment records.

b. No Policymaking or Ideological Authority
Personnel offices shall not exercise policymaking authority, interpret law, enforce ideological standards, or impose academic, political, or moral frameworks as conditions of employment or service.

c. Prohibition on Quasi-Judicial Discipline
Personnel offices shall not adjudicate rights, determine guilt, impose punishment, or conduct disciplinary proceedings beyond ministerial processing of decisions lawfully made by competent authority.
Disciplinary determinations affecting rights or status shall be subject to due process and, where required, judicial review.

d. Substance Over Form
Employment actions shall be evaluated by their practical effect, not by title, classification, terminology, or internal designation.
Reclassification, reassignment, restructuring, or alteration of duties shall not be used to evade constitutional protections, statutory limits, or accountability obligations.

e. Neutral Administration of Employment Standards
Employment standards shall be job-related, functionally defined, and uniformly applied.
Personnel offices shall not create, modify, or enforce standards beyond those lawfully enacted.

f. Recordkeeping and Transparency
All personnel actions, classifications, evaluations, and employment decisions shall be documented, preserved, and subject to transparency and review consistent with Article XI.

g. Liability for Abuse
Any personnel official who knowingly participates in concealment, misclassification, retaliation, or circumvention of constitutional or statutory protections commits a breach of public trust and is subject to personal liability as provided by law.


Section 5. Prohibition on Ideological Governance

a. Neutral Execution of Law
Administrative bodies and civil servants shall execute the law neutrally and without ideological bias.
No office, agency, or official may advance, impose, or enforce political, moral, philosophical, academic, or social ideologies through administrative action.

b. No Ideological Conditions of Employment or Service
Employment, promotion, evaluation, retention, or access to public services shall not be conditioned upon adherence to, affirmation of, or participation in any ideological belief, doctrine, or expressive framework.

c. Prohibition on Compelled Speech and Belief
No civil servant or member of the public shall be compelled, directly or indirectly, to express agreement with, participate in, or endorse any ideological position as a condition of employment, benefit, or lawful interaction with the administrative state.

d. Training and Professional Development Limits
Training, education, and professional development provided or required by administrative bodies shall be limited to job-related skills, lawful procedures, safety, ethics grounded in law, and functional competence.
Ideological instruction, belief conditioning, or values-based programming unrelated to lawful job performance is prohibited.

e. No Governance by Internal Theory or Framework
Administrative bodies shall not govern through internal theoretical models, academic constructs, or professional frameworks not enacted into law.
Such constructs may inform internal study but shall not be binding on employees or the public.

f. Enforcement and Nullification
Any administrative action, policy, or employment decision taken in violation of this Section is void.
Affected individuals shall be entitled to relief and remedies as provided by law and by Article XI.
g. Protection of Lawful Expression
Nothing in this Article shall be construed to prohibit civil servants from providing truthful information, factual testimony, technical explanation, or lawful advice when requested by competent authority or required by law.


Section 6. Neutrality, Merit, and Appointment

a. Merit Defined
Merit in civil service appointment, promotion, and retention shall be defined by demonstrable competence, qualifications, experience, and performance directly related to the lawful duties of the position.

b. Prohibition on Ideological or Extraneous Criteria
No appointment, promotion, evaluation, or retention decision may be based upon political affiliation, ideological belief, academic doctrine, moral viewpoint, demographic classification, or any criterion unrelated to lawful job performance.

c. Transparent Appointment Processes
Appointments to civil service positions shall be made through transparent, reviewable processes designed to ensure fairness, competence, and accountability.
Selection criteria and decision rationales shall be documented and preserved.

d. No Credentialism as Authority
Professional credentials, academic degrees, or certifications shall not confer independent authority or policymaking power.
Credentials may qualify an individual for service but shall not substitute for lawful authorization.

e. Equal Application of Standards
Merit standards shall be applied uniformly to all similarly situated candidates and employees.
Preferential treatment, exemptions, or disparate standards are prohibited absent explicit legal authorization.

f. Prohibition on Loyalty Tests
No civil servant shall be required to demonstrate loyalty to any individual, party, ideology, institution, or internal doctrine beyond loyalty to the Constitution and the law.

g. Accountability in Appointment Authority
Officials responsible for appointment and promotion decisions are accountable for compliance with this Section.
Knowingly violating merit or neutrality requirements constitutes a breach of public trust.

Section 7. Discipline, Due Process, and the Boundary of Punishment

a. Administrative Discipline Distinguished
Administrative discipline exists solely to ensure lawful performance of duties, integrity of operations, and continuity of service.
It shall not be punitive in nature and shall not substitute for criminal, civil, or constitutional adjudication.

b. Due Process in Administrative Discipline
Any civil servant subject to disciplinary action is entitled to notice of the alleged misconduct, a clear statement of applicable standards, an opportunity to respond, and a reasoned decision based on the record.
Discipline imposed without due process is void.

c. Limits of Disciplinary Authority
Administrative bodies may impose only those disciplinary measures expressly authorized by law and proportionate to administrative error or misconduct.
Sanctions shall not extend beyond employment status, duties, or lawful conditions of service.

d. Boundary Between Discipline and Punishment
Where conduct reasonably indicates criminal behavior, constitutional violation, corruption, retaliation, or abuse of power, the matter shall be referred to the Constitutional Review Court and appropriate prosecutorial authority.
Administrative bodies shall not conceal, resolve internally, or substitute discipline for adjudication of such conduct.

e. Prohibition on Internal Adjudication of Rights
Administrative bodies shall not adjudicate guilt, innocence, liability, or constitutional rights.
Any attempt to do so constitutes an unlawful assumption of judicial power.

f. No Retaliatory Discipline
Disciplinary authority shall not be exercised in retaliation for lawful conduct, including whistleblowing, refusal of unlawful orders, protected speech, or participation in oversight or judicial processes.
Retaliatory discipline is void and constitutes a breach of public trust.
The Constitutional Review Court may order immediate reinstatement, back pay, and injunctive relief.

g. Recordkeeping and Review
All disciplinary actions shall be documented, preserved, and subject to review consistent with Article XI.
Secret, informal, or undocumented discipline is prohibited.

Section 8. Refusal of Unlawful Orders

a. Duty to Refuse
Civil servants have an affirmative duty to refuse any order, directive, instruction, or request that is unlawful, unconstitutional, or beyond the authority of the issuing official or body.

b. No Penalty for Lawful Refusal
No civil servant shall suffer adverse action, discipline, retaliation, or disadvantage for refusing to carry out an unlawful or unconstitutional order.
Any such adverse action is void.

c. Documentation and Reporting
A civil servant who refuses an unlawful order shall document the refusal and may report the matter to the Constitutional Judicial Commission, the Constitutional Review Court, or other lawful investigative authorities without fear of reprisal.

d. No Reliance on Superior Orders
Obedience to superior orders does not excuse unlawful action.
Civil servants remain personally accountable for compliance with constitutional and legal limits.

e. Protection of Good-Faith Judgment
Where the unlawfulness of an order is reasonably disputed, good-faith refusal or delay pending clarification or review shall be protected conduct.

f. Escalation of Systemic Abuse
Patterns of unlawful orders, coercion to comply, or retaliation for refusal shall constitute evidence of institutional abuse and shall constitute evidence of institutional abuse and shall be certified to the Constitutional Judicial Commission, which may petition the Constitutional Review Court for remediation or dissolution.

Section 9. Agency Structure, Scope, and Sunset

a. Defined Scope of Authority
Each administrative agency shall operate within a clearly defined scope of authority established by law.
Functions, jurisdiction, and limits shall be expressly stated and publicly accessible.

b. No Permanent Expansion
No administrative agency may expand its mission, jurisdiction, staffing, budgetary scope, or operational authority beyond that expressly authorized by law.
Expansion by custom, internal directive, interagency agreement, or external partnership is prohibited.

c. Temporary and Task-Specific Bodies
Administrative task forces, special units, or temporary bodies may be established only for defined purposes and durations.
Such bodies shall dissolve automatically upon completion of their lawful mandate.

d. Mandatory Sunset and Reauthorization
Administrative agencies exercising regulatory, enforcement, or coercive authority shall be subject to periodic sunset and reauthorization by the Legislature.
Failure to reauthorize results in automatic suspension or dissolution of authority.

e. Prohibition on Self-Perpetuation
No agency may perpetuate itself through internal rulemaking, procedural entrenchment, emergency declarations, or claims of indispensability.
Institutional continuity shall not substitute for lawful authorization.
The Constitutional Judicial Commission shall have standing to audit and certify violations of this Section.
f. Consolidation and Dissolution Authority
The Legislature may consolidate, restructure, or dissolve administrative agencies to prevent redundancy, capture, or inefficiency.
Administrative resistance to lawful consolidation or dissolution constitutes a breach of public trust.

g. Public Disclosure of Structure
The organizational structure, leadership, reporting lines, and delegated authorities of each agency shall be publicly disclosed and maintained as a matter of record.
h. Transitional Administration
Upon lawful dissolution, consolidation, or restructuring of an administrative body, reasonable transitional measures may be employed to wind down operations and protect vested rights lawfully accrued prior to dissolution.

No new rights, authorities, or benefits may accrue under an abolished or suspended authority.


Section 10. Recordkeeping, Transparency, and Public Interface

a. Duty of Public Accessibility
Administrative bodies shall conduct their operations in a manner accessible to the public.
Information regarding agency functions, procedures, points of contact, and decision pathways shall be made readily available in clear and comprehensible form.

b. Recordkeeping Obligations
All administrative actions, decisions, communications, policies, directives, and employment actions shall be recorded, preserved, and indexed as public records consistent with Article XI.
Failure to maintain accurate records constitutes a breach of public trust.

c. Transparency of Decision Processes
Administrative bodies shall disclose the criteria, standards, and legal authority governing their actions.
Opaque, discretionary, or unexplained administrative decisions affecting rights, duties, or benefits are prohibited.

d. Prohibition on Informal or Shadow Processes
No administrative action may be taken through informal, undocumented, or unofficial processes.
Customary practice, internal consensus, or unwritten procedure shall not substitute for lawful authority or public record.

e. Public Right to Explanation
Any person materially affected by an administrative action is entitled to a clear explanation of the legal basis, factual grounds, and decision pathway underlying that action.

f. Accessibility and Non-Obstruction
Administrative bodies shall not obstruct, delay, or frustrate public access through unnecessary complexity, procedural barriers, or technical design.
Administrative systems shall be designed to facilitate understanding, not to deter inquiry.

g. Supremacy of Transparency Obligations
Claims of administrative convenience, efficiency, confidentiality, or professional discretion shall not defeat the transparency and recordkeeping obligations imposed by this Article and by Article XI.

Section 11. Accountability, Removal, and Liability

a. Personal Accountability
Civil servants and administrative officials are personally accountable for the lawful exercise of their authority.
No office, title, or institutional role shields an individual from responsibility for unlawful, unconstitutional, or bad-faith administrative action.

b. Removal for Cause
Civil servants may be removed from office for cause, including breach of fiduciary duty, abuse of authority, corruption, retaliation, concealment, or knowing violation of constitutional or statutory limits.
Removal decisions shall be documented and subject to review as provided by law.

c. No Institutional Immunity
Administrative bodies shall not claim institutional immunity to shield individuals from accountability.
Collective decision-making does not absolve personal responsibility.

d. Escalation to Legal Liability
Where administrative conduct constitutes criminal behavior, constitutional violation, or grave abuse of public trust, the matter shall be referred for prosecution or judicial adjudication pursuant to Article VII and Article VI.
Administrative resolution shall not preclude legal accountability.

e. Joint and Several Liability
Where unlawful administrative action results from coordinated conduct, all responsible participants are jointly and severally liable to the extent of their involvement.

f. No Indemnification for Bad-Faith Acts
Public funds shall not be used to indemnify, defend, or reimburse civil servants for liability arising from willful, malicious, or bad-faith violations of law or constitutional duty.

g. Record of Accountability
Findings of misconduct, removal, or liability shall be preserved as public records consistent with Article XI.
Concealment or expungement of such records is prohibited.

Section 12. Non-Delegation and Anti-Proxy Rule

a. Non-Delegation of Administrative Authority
Administrative authority shall not be delegated, transferred, or outsourced to private entities, nongovernmental organizations, contractors, consultants, foundations, academic institutions, or international bodies except as expressly authorized by law for limited, ministerial functions.

b. No Proxy Governance
No administrative body may govern, regulate, enforce, surveil, adjudicate, or otherwise exercise public authority through proxies or intermediaries in order to evade constitutional, statutory, or transparency obligations.

c. Functional Test of Authority
Where a private or non-governmental entity performs functions that materially influence or determine public outcomes, such entity shall be deemed to exercise public authority and shall be subject to all constitutional duties, limitations, and liabilities applicable to administrative bodies.

d. Accountability Parity
Entities exercising delegated administrative functions are subject to the same standards of transparency, recordkeeping, neutrality, fiduciary duty, and accountability as the delegating authority.
No lesser standard shall apply by reason of contractual form or organizational status.

e. Prohibition on Ideological, Technical, or Academic Proxies
Administrative authority shall not be exercised through ideological programs, technical platforms, academic frameworks, professional standards, or automated systems that substitute private judgment for lawful public decision-making.

f. Termination and Debarment
Any delegation or proxy arrangement undertaken in violation of this Section is void.
Entities participating in such arrangements are subject to termination of contracts, permanent debarment from public authority, and liability as provided by law.

Section 13. Interpretation and Supremacy

a. Rule of Construction
This Article shall be interpreted to preserve neutrality in administration, restraint in authority, and subordination of administrative power to lawfully enacted decisions of the constitutional branches.

b. Substance Over Form
Courts and oversight bodies shall evaluate administrative conduct by its practical effect, not by title, classification, internal designation, or procedural form.

c. No Deference to Administrative Assertion
Assertions of expertise, professionalism, efficiency, necessity, or institutional competence shall not be afforded deference where administrative action is challenged under this Article.

d. Integrated Constitutional Reading
This Article shall be read in harmony with Articles I, V, VI, VII, and XI.
Where conflict arises, constitutional limits on power, transparency, accountability, and the rights of the People shall control.

e. Anti-Evasion Principle
Formal compliance that defeats the purpose of this Article constitutes violation.
Administrative practices designed to obscure responsibility, dilute accountability, or entrench authority are prohibited.

f. Self-Execution
This Article is self-executing in all its parts.
Failure of legislative or executive implementation shall not delay, suspend, or diminish its force.
