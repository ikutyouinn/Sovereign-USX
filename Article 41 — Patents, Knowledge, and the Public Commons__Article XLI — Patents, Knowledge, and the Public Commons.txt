Section 1. Principle of Instrumental Intellectual Property

Intellectual property, including patents and related exclusive rights, is not a natural right inherent to persons or entities.

Such rights exist solely as temporary legal privileges, granted by the Republic for the limited purpose of encouraging disclosure, innovation, investment, and the advancement of knowledge for the eventual benefit of the People.

No grant of intellectual property shall be construed as perpetual, absolute, or immune from limitation, convergence, or termination in the public interest, as governed by this Constitution.

Section 2. Disclosure as the Condition of Protection

Exclusive intellectual property protection shall be conditioned upon full, truthful, and sufficient public disclosure of the protected invention, method, or process.

No person or entity may simultaneously claim exclusive rights while withholding, obscuring, fragmenting, or strategically concealing essential information necessary for understanding, replication, evaluation, or future improvement of the protected subject matter.

All disclosures required under this Article are incorporated within and subject to the archival, access, preservation, and anti-suppression duties established in Article X (Knowledge, Technology, and the Digital Commons).

Failure to disclose as required shall render any claimed exclusivity voidable or unenforceable, subject to judicial determination.

Section 3. Duration, Recoupment, and Convergence

Exclusive intellectual property protections shall be limited in duration and structured to permit reasonable opportunity for recoupment of development costs and profit, without enabling permanent control, hoarding, or suppression of knowledge.

Congress may establish statutory frameworks that:

a. define finite periods of exclusivity proportionate to the nature, risk, and cost of development;
b. recognize earlier convergence where public funding, civilization-scale impact, or rapid cost recovery is demonstrated;
c. provide for voluntary licensing incentives following recoupment of reasonable investment and profit;
d. ensure that, upon expiration or termination, protected knowledge transitions into the public commons.

All intellectual property granted under this Article remains subject to the mandatory convergence, preservation, and public-access provisions of Article X, including acceleration or termination upon abandonment, dissolution, suppression, or abuse.

No alteration of exclusivity shall apply retroactively to vested grants absent just compensation or voluntary assent.

Section 4. Archival Preservation and Knowledge Continuity

All inventions, processes, methods, schematics, source materials, and supporting documentation disclosed pursuant to this Article shall be preserved in a secure, durable, and publicly accountable archival system, as governed by Article X (Knowledge, Technology, and the Digital Commons).

Such preservation shall ensure:

a. long-term integrity, accessibility, and readability;
b. protection against loss, destruction, manipulation, or strategic disappearance;
c. continuity of knowledge across generations, institutions, and technological transitions.

No grant of intellectual property shall exempt the holder from archival obligations imposed by Article X.

Section 5. Suppression, Shelving, and Abuse of Privilege

Intellectual property privileges shall not be used to suppress, bury, delay, or strategically withhold knowledge whose disclosure would materially advance scientific understanding, public welfare, or civilizational capacity.

Abuse of privilege includes, but is not limited to:

a. acquisition of exclusive rights for the primary purpose of non-use or delay;
b. defensive patenting intended to prevent deployment or competition without bona fide development;
c. fragmentation of disclosures to obscure functional understanding;
d. contractual or licensing schemes designed to evade convergence to the public commons.

Findings of suppression or abuse under this Article shall require clear and convincing evidence and may result in limitation, termination, or acceleration of convergence, consistent with Article X enforcement standards and subject to judicial review.

Any person or enterprise with standing may seek judicial relief against suppression, shelving, or abuse of intellectual property privileges, subject to evidentiary standards consistent with this Constitution.

Section 6. Patent Office Stewardship and Anti-Capture Constraints

The Patent Office or successor authority shall function solely as a registrar, archivist, and steward of disclosure, and not as a gatekeeper of innovation, industrial policy actor, or market participant.

Its duties shall be limited to:

a. registering disclosures and claims of exclusivity;
b. ensuring compliance with disclosure and archival requirements;
c. recording statutory triggers for expiration, convergence, or termination.

The Patent Office shall be subject to strict transparency, conflict-of-interest, and anti-capture rules, consistent with Articles X and XI, and shall not exercise discretionary authority to favor, suppress, delay, or condition innovation beyond mechanical compliance with law.

Section 7. Public Funding, Research, and Accelerated Convergence

Where research, development, or innovation is funded in whole or in part by public resources, any resulting intellectual property shall be subject to accelerated convergence to the public commons.

Congress may provide by law that publicly funded or publicly commissioned innovations:

a. receive reduced periods of exclusivity;
b. require broader disclosure obligations at earlier stages;
c. transition to the public commons upon recoupment of reasonable private investment, where applicable.

Public funding shall not be used to create permanent monopolies, proprietary choke points, or exclusive control over knowledge whose development was materially supported by the People.

No alteration of exclusivity arising under this Section shall apply retroactively to vested grants absent just compensation or voluntary assent.

Section 8. Public Records, Access, and Auditability

All intellectual property disclosures, registrations, archival materials, and convergence determinations under this Article shall constitute public records, subject to access, inspection, and audit consistent with Article XXXIX (Records Access) and Article X (Knowledge, Technology, and the Digital Commons).

Limitations on access may be permitted only where narrowly necessary to protect:

a. legitimate national security interests;
b. personal privacy unrelated to the protected subject matter;
c. temporary competitive confidentiality expressly authorized by law.

Such limitations shall be construed narrowly and shall not be used to conceal suppression, abuse, capture, or evasion of convergence.

Section 9. Construction

This Article shall be interpreted to:

a. favor disclosure, competition, and innovation over secrecy and rent-seeking;
b. ensure that intellectual property serves the public good rather than private enclosure;
c. prevent permanent monopolization or suppression of civilization-advancing knowledge;
d. preserve incentives for creation while guaranteeing eventual convergence to the public commons;
e. permit private parties with standing to enforce the prohibitions of this Article through judicial process;
f. operate in harmony with Articles X, XI, and XXXIX, which govern knowledge stewardship, anti-capture safeguards, and public records.

Nothing in this Article shall be construed to require the continuation of any intellectual property regime beyond such time as the People, through their representatives, determine that such privileges are no longer necessary to incentivize innovation.
